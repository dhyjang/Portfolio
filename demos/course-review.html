Inside a course quality review

Course review is only as valuable as the feedback it produces. Below is a fictional module page with four things a trained reviewer would notice, followed by how a real review closes the loop. Each note follows the same craft: cite the evidence, explain the principle, and suggest a change the instructor can act on — with a worked example where possible, because "here's what that could look like" lands better than "consider improving this." And reviews open with strengths, because faculty are partners, not subjects.

1 · Instructor self-assessment→ 2 · Review→ 3 · Report→ 4 · Enhancements→ 5 · Verification & certification

Before the reviewer ever opens the course, the instructor completes a worksheet and self-assessment against the same checklist the reviewer will use — documenting context (like whether objectives are instructor-written or state-mandated, which changes what a fair finding looks like), flagging known student pain points, and naming what they'd like targeted feedback on. The review starts with the instructor's voice, not the reviewer's judgment.

PSY 101 · Sample Course (fictional)

Module 3: Memory and Learning

Module Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Understand the three stages of memory
  • Compare classical and operant conditioning using real-world examples
Reviewer note · Measurable objectives

Evidence: The first objective uses "understand," while the second uses "compare … using real-world examples."

Why it matters: "Understand" can't be observed or assessed — there's no way for a student to know when they've achieved it, or for the quiz to measure it. The second objective is a great model: the verb is observable and the criterion is built in.

Suggestion: Consider revising to match the strength of your second objective — for example, "Describe the three stages of memory and give an example of each." Your Module 3 quiz already asks students to do exactly this, so the revision would bring the objective in line with an assessment that's already well designed.

This Week's Activities

  • Read Chapter 6 (pp. 180–214)
  • Watch: "How Memories Form" video lecture (18 min)
  • Complete the Memory Palace practice exercise
Reviewer note · Media accessibility

Evidence: The video lecture plays without captions, and no transcript is linked on the page.

Why it matters: Captions are essential for students who are deaf or hard of hearing — and they benefit far more students than that: anyone studying in a noisy place, watching without headphones, or processing English as an additional language.

Suggestion: The platform's auto-captioning gets you 80% of the way in one click; a 15-minute cleanup pass handles the rest. If it would help, the captioning workshop covers the exact workflow for videos like this one — and I'm happy to walk through the first one together.

Reviewer note · Instructions & learner support

Evidence: The Memory Palace exercise asks students to "submit your completed palace," but the page doesn't say what format to submit, where, or what a strong submission looks like.

Why it matters: Ambiguous logistics generate a predictable wave of "what do I actually turn in?" emails — and quietly disadvantage first-generation students, who are least likely to ask.

Suggestion: Two sentences would close the gap: the expected format and length, and a link to one example. The strong example you already share in your week-3 announcement would work perfectly here — moving it onto the page puts it where students look first.

Graded Discussion: Memory in Your Life

Post about a time a memory strategy helped or failed you. Reply to two classmates. Worth 20 points.

Reviewer note · Grading transparency

Evidence: The discussion is worth 20 points, but no criteria explain how those points are earned — is a two-sentence reply worth the same as a substantive one?

Why it matters: When students can't see how work is evaluated, grading feels arbitrary even when it isn't — and discussion quality drops to the minimum that seems safe.

Suggestion: Even a three-line breakdown (initial post 10 pts, each reply 5 pts, with one sentence on what "substantive" means) changes behavior. If you'd like, the simple discussion rubric template takes about ten minutes to adapt and attaches directly to the assignment.

The other half of the review

From findings to certification

A report isn't the end of a review — it's the midpoint. Here's the structure the full review follows (fictional data, real process):

Course strengths — always first

  • Consistent module structure with content sectioned by topic for easy navigation
  • Instructor-created walkthrough videos that set clear expectations for asynchronous learners
  • Weekly overview pages that frame what's ahead before stating the objectives

Prioritized action items

  • High — Revise the "understand" objective to a measurable verb (worked example provided in the note) · target: 2 weeks
  • Medium — Add grading criteria to the discussion · target: 2 weeks
  • Low — Caption the video lecture and post logistics for the practice exercise · target: next term prep

Verification review — closing the loop

Ten days after the report, the reviewer returns to the course, confirms each agreed item is implemented, and signs off. The instructor gets certification, not just critique — and the course gets a scheduled future re-review date so quality stays a cycle, not a one-time event.
